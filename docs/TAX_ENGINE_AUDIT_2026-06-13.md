### ✅ 1. 🔴 Federal standard deduction double-deducted for CO/MT/ND/SC (federal-taxable base states) — FIXED (c493eecd)

**Domain:** state-income · **Severity:** critical · **Category:** incorrect-calculation · **Location:** `src/lib/tax/state-income/compute.ts:157-162, 221, 226`

- **Current behavior:** For states whose income base is `federal-taxable` (CO, MT, ND, SC per income-base.ts), `startingIncome` is set to `federalIncome.taxableIncome` — i.e. federal taxable income, which is already net of the federal standard deduction. The code then unconditionally subtracts a state std deduction via `getStdDeduction(...)` at line 221 and `stateTaxableIncome = Math.max(0, stateAGI - stdDed - personalExemptionDeduction)` at line 226. std-deductions.ts returns 16,100/32,200 for CO/MT/ND and 8,350/16,700 for SC for 2026, so the federal std deduction is effectively removed twice. Verified against goldens: CO wage = (85000-16100)*0.044 = 3031.6 (golden 3031.6), MT = 3441.6 (golden), ND = 398.2875 (golden), SC = 3942.9 (golden) — all match the double-deducted figure.
- **Correct behavior:** These states start their return FROM federal taxable income (CO DR-0104 line 1, MT Form 2, ND-1, SC1040 all begin at federal 1040 taxable income) and provide NO separate state standard deduction on top. The state std deduction should be 0 (or the federal-taxable base should be grossed back up to AGI before applying a state deduction). Correct CO wage = 85000*0.044 = 3740; SC ≈ 4443.9; ND ≈ 712.24; MT ≈ 4351.25.
- **Tax-law basis:** CO Rev. Stat. §39-22-104 (CO taxable income = federal taxable income ± modifications, no separate std deduction); MT §15-30-2120 (2024 reform: starts from federal taxable income); ND Form ND-1 line 1; SC Code §12-6-560/SC1040 line 1. The std-deductions.ts notes themselves say 'Federal std deduction included in income base (starts from federal taxable income)'.
- **Impact:** Hits every CO/MT/ND/SC resident every projection year. Understates state tax by roughly stdDeduction × marginal rate: ~$708/yr (CO single), ~$910/yr (MT single mid-income), ~$314/yr (ND), ~$501/yr (SC) for a single filer; roughly double for MFJ. Compounds over a multi-decade projection.
- **Suggested fix:** In compute.ts, gate the std deduction on the income base so federal-taxable states get zero: e.g. `const stdDed = baseRule.base === "federal-taxable" ? 0 : getStdDeduction(input.state, input.year, stateFs, input.primaryAge, input.spouseAge);` (the WA cap-gains exclusion path is unaffected because WA is state-gti and uses its own getStdDeduction call in the short-circuit). Equivalently, set CO/MT/ND/SC std-deduction rows to 0 for 2025 and 2026 in std-deductions.ts. Keep SC's separate retirement/age-65 subtractions and ND's zero-rate brackets untouched. Then regenerate the golden JSON snapshots (scripts/generate-state-income-goldens.local.ts) and verify the deltas equal exactly stdDeduction × marginal-rate for these four states only.

### ✅ 2. 🔴 Rhode Island uses wrong tax mechanism and rates — §2011 table on whole estate, not exemption-anchored 0.8% brackets — FIXED (c493eecd)

**Domain:** state-estate-inheritance · **Severity:** critical · **Category:** incorrect-calculation · **Location:** `src/lib/tax/state-estate/data.ts:187-203`

- **Current behavior:** RI is modeled as a standard exemption-anchored graduated schedule: brackets run from the $1,802,431 exemption at 0.8%, 1.6%, 2.4%, 3.2%, 4.0%, then jump to 16%. computeStateEstateTax treats RI like HI/OR/ME — applyBrackets(rule.brackets, baseForTax) taxes only the amount over the exemption at these low rates. Golden test asserts RI($2,000,000) → $1,580.55 (= $197,569 × 0.8%).
- **Correct behavior:** RI computes tax via the pre-2002 IRC §2011 maximum-state-death-credit table applied to the WHOLE taxable estate from $0, then subtracts a flat credit ($74,300 for 2025, $87,940 for 2026). This is identical in structure to the MA fixedCredit mechanism. The marginal rate on the first dollar above the ~$1.8M exemption is 7.2% (the §2011 tier at $1.5M–$2M), not 0.8%, rising to 16%. RI($2,000,000) ≈ §2011 table on $2M ($99,600) − $74,300 credit ≈ $25,300, vs the code's $1,580.55.
- **Tax-law basis:** RI Gen. Laws §44-22-1.1 (tax = maximum §2011 state-death-tax credit in effect 1/1/2001, applied to the whole estate, less the RI credit amount); RI Div. of Taxation ADV 2025-27 (2026 credit $87,940, threshold $1,838,056). Marginal rates 7.2%–16%.
- **Impact:** Hits every RI-resident decedent. Understatement is roughly an order of magnitude in the lower bands (e.g. ~$23,700 too low at a $2M estate) and grows: a $5.8M estate is taxed at ≤4% under the code vs the §2011 table's much higher effective rate. For mid-size RI estates the state estate tax can be understated by hundreds of thousands of dollars.
- **Suggested fix:** Model RI exactly like MA in src/lib/tax/state-estate/data.ts: set fixedCredit: 87_940 (2026 RI credit per ADV 2025-27), drop the exemption-anchored 0.8%→16% brackets, and use the full pre-2002 IRC §2011 graduated table anchored at $40,000 (the identical table MA uses at lines 98-117 of data.ts). The existing compute.ts fixedCredit path (lines 107-109, 160) then applies the §2011 table to the whole estate and subtracts the credit with a $0 floor. Set the RI threshold/exemption field for documentation/notes to $1,838,056 (the value at which table tax equals the $87,940 credit). Update the golden RI cases: RI($1,838,056)→$0 (credit fully absorbs the table tax), RI($2,000,000)→ §2011-table($2M) − $87,940 ≈ $15,980 (verify the exact table value with the same table the MA cases use). Note: since the brackets become $0-anchored, RI is no longer 'exemption-anchored' for projectIndexedRule — like MA it should not be in the indexed bracket-shift path; if indexed inflation projection of the threshold/credit is desired, project the fixedCredit and threshold, not the table brackets (the §2011 table is statutorily frozen at 1/1/2001 and does not index). Also flag separately: data.ts uses 2025 values (exemption 1,802,431, effectiveYear 2025) while the engine runs 2026 deaths — refresh to the 2026 ADV 2025-27 figures.

### ✅ 3. 🔴 Washington SB 5813 bracket schedule is wrong — missing 10% bottom tier and incorrect upper-tier rates/breakpoints — FIXED (c493eecd; modeled via ESB 6347 going-forward schedule, see #20)

**Domain:** state-estate-inheritance · **Severity:** critical · **Category:** incorrect-calculation · **Location:** `src/lib/tax/state-estate/data.ts:219-227`

- **Current behavior:** WA brackets are anchored at the $3M exemption and tax the amount over it as: $3–4M @15%, $4–5M @17%, $5–6M @19%, $6–7M @22%, $7–8M @25%, $8–9M @30%, $9M+ @35%. Golden test asserts WA($4,000,000) → $150,000 (= $1M over exemption × 15%).
- **Correct behavior:** Under SB 5813 the graduated rates apply to the 'Washington taxable estate' (amount over the $3M exemption) starting at 10%, on these tiers: $0–1M @10%, $1–2M @15%, $2–3M @17%, $3–4M @19%, $4–6M @23%, $6–7M @26%, $7–9M @30%, $9M+ @35%. So the first $1M over the exemption is 10% (not 15%); the $4–6M-over band is a single 23% (code splits it 17%/19% and is too low); $6–7M-over is 26% (code 22%); $7–9M-over is 30% (code splits 25%/30%). Correct WA($4,000,000) = $1M (WA taxable estate) × 10% = $100,000, not $150,000.
- **Tax-law basis:** WA SB 5813 (ESSB 5813, eff. 7/1/2025), RCW 83.100 rate table: 10/15/17/19/23/26/30/35% on tiers of the Washington taxable estate beginning at $0 above the $3M applicable exclusion.
- **Impact:** Affects every WA-resident decedent with a taxable estate. The missing 10% tier and shifted breakpoints cause errors in both directions but are net-material; e.g. at $4M the code overstates by $50K, while at $6.5M the code's 22% vs law's 26% understates the top band. WA is the highest-rate estate-tax state, so dollar errors are large.
- **Suggested fix:** Replace the WA brackets in src/lib/tax/state-estate/data.ts:219-227 with the SB 5813 schedule, anchored at the absolute estate value (each WA-taxable-estate tier shifted up by the $3M exemption):
[
  { from: 3_000_000, to: 4_000_000, rate: 0.10 },
  { from: 4_000_000, to: 5_000_000, rate: 0.15 },
  { from: 5_000_000, to: 6_000_000, rate: 0.17 },
  { from: 6_000_000, to: 7_000_000, rate: 0.19 },
  { from: 7_000_000, to: 9_000_000, rate: 0.23 },
  { from: 9_000_000, to: 10_000_000, rate: 0.26 },
  { from: 10_000_000, to: 12_000_000, rate: 0.30 },
  { from: 12_000_000, to: null, rate: 0.35 },
]
Then update BOTH WA golden cases in all-states.test.ts: WA $4M -> $100,000 (was $150,000) and WA $12M -> $1,930,000 (was $2,330,000), and fix the inline comment on line 69 accordingly. Note the projectIndexedRule shift-by-delta logic at compute.ts:267-274 already preserves this exemption-anchored structure for out-year indexed deaths, so no change is needed there.

### ✅ 4. 🟠 Preferential (LTCG + qual-div) base not capped to taxable income — over-taxes gains when deductions exceed ordinary income — FIXED (c493eecd)

**Domain:** federal-ordinary · **Severity:** high · **Category:** incorrect-calculation · **Location:** `src/lib/tax/calculate.ts:77-88`

- **Current behavior:** incomeTaxBase = max(0, taxableIncome − capitalGains − dividends) is floored at 0 when below-line deductions exceed ordinary income, which is correct for the ordinary base. But calcCapGainsTax is then called with the FULL gain amount: calcCapGainsTax(capitalGains + dividends, incomeTaxBase, ...). The preferentially-taxed amount is never reduced by the portion of the standard/itemized deduction that spilled past ordinary income onto the net capital gain. Verified by running the engine: MFJ 2026, ordinaryIncome=0, LTCG=150000, std deduction 32200 → taxableIncome=117800, incomeTaxBase=0, but capitalGainsTax computes 7620 (0% on 99200, 15% on 50800).
- **Correct behavior:** Per the Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet, the amount taxed at preferential 0/15/20% rates is the SMALLER of (net capital gain + qualified dividends) and (taxable income). The deduction first offsets ordinary income, and any excess offsets net capital gain, shrinking the preferentially-taxed amount. In the example the preferential base should be min(150000, 117800)=117800 → 0% on 99200, 15% on 18600 = 2790, not 7620 (overcharge of $4,830).
- **Tax-law basis:** IRC §1(h); Form 1040 Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet (line 1 = taxable income; the preferential amount is bounded by taxable income, lines 7/18 net it against the ordinary portion). Schedule D Tax Worksheet line 1/7.
- **Impact:** Bites any household where below-line deductions exceed ordinary income and there is material LTCG/qual-div — classic low-spend retiree harvesting appreciated taxable-account gains, or an early-retirement gain-harvesting year. Error scales with how far the over-deduction pushes gains across the 0%/15% (or 15%/20%) line; the demonstrated MFJ case overcharges $4,830, and larger gain-harvest years can be several thousand dollars per year, compounding across a multi-decade projection and distorting Roth-conversion / gain-harvest optimization.
- **Suggested fix:** Clamp the preferential base to taxable income before taxing, in calculate.ts. Replace the line-84 call with:

  const preferentialBase = Math.min(capitalGains + dividends, taxableIncome);
  const capitalGainsTax = calcCapGainsTax(preferentialBase, incomeTaxBase, p.capGainsBrackets[fs]);

incomeTaxBase already floors at 0, so the stacking floor stays correct; the clamp makes incomeTaxBase + preferentialBase == taxableIncome in all cases, matching the QDCGT worksheet. Note the AMT path (calculate.ts lines 104–109) passes the same unclamped ltcgPlusQdiv = capitalGains + dividends into calcAmtTentative with regularOrdinaryBase = incomeTaxBase, so it has the identical over-taxation bug for the AMT cap-gains split; apply the same min(gain, taxableIncome) clamp there (or pass preferentialBase) for consistency. Add regression tests: (1) MFJ ordinary=0/LTCG=150000 expecting capitalGainsTax≈2790; (2) partial spill ordinary=10000/LTCG=150000 to lock the invariant.

### ✅ 5. 🟠 Preferentially-taxed amount not capped at taxable income — deductions/QBI spilling into LTCG over-tax the gains — FIXED (c493eecd; same fix as #4)

**Domain:** cap-gains · **Severity:** high · **Category:** incorrect-calculation · **Location:** `src/lib/tax/calculate.ts:76-88`

- **Current behavior:** Step 7 computes incomeTaxBase = max(0, taxableIncome - capitalGains - dividends) and floors it at 0 when deductions exceed ordinary income. But step 9 then calls calcCapGainsTax(capitalGains + dividends, incomeTaxBase, ...) passing the FULL GROSS LTCG+QDI as the amount to be preferentially taxed. When below-line deductions (standard/itemized) — or QBI — exceed the ordinary income, those deductions spill into the capital-gain layer, yet the engine still taxes the entire gross LTCG+QDI. Example (MFJ 2026): ordinary 20,000, LTCG+QDI 100,000, std deduction ~30,000 → taxableIncome 90,000, incomeTaxBase floored to 0. Engine taxes the full 100,000: 0% on 99,200 + 15% on 800 = $120. Per the Schedule D Tax Worksheet only min(100,000, 90,000)=90,000 of gain is taxable, all within the 0% bracket → $0. Larger spread case (ordinary 30,000, LTCG+QDI 500,000, itemized 200,000): engine $60,120 vs correct $34,620, a $25,500 overstatement.
- **Correct behavior:** Per the Schedule D Tax Worksheet (and Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet), the amount eligible for preferential rates is the SMALLER of (net capital gain + qualified dividends) or taxable income. Deductions that exceed ordinary income reduce the LTCG layer dollar-for-dollar, so those absorbed gains are never taxed. The preferential amount must be capped: min(capitalGains + dividends, taxableIncome).
- **Tax-law basis:** IRC §1(h)(1) ('net capital gain' is taxed within taxable income); IRS Schedule D Tax Worksheet line 1 (taxable income) / lines 9-10 (smaller of gain or taxable income); Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet line 6 = line 1 (taxable income) minus the preferential amount, never below 0.
- **Impact:** Bites whenever below-line deductions (or QBI) exceed ordinary income — extremely common for retirees / early-retirees living mostly off long-term gains and qualified dividends with little wage/RMD income, and for anyone with large itemized deductions (big charitable years, large SALT, mortgage) relative to ordinary income. Over-taxes LTCG that should be 0%/15%; dollar error ranges from a few hundred (gains near the 0% top) to $25k+ (large gains where the absorbed slice would have been 15%/20%). Always errs toward overstating tax.
- **Suggested fix:** In calculate.ts step 9 only, cap the preferential amount at taxable income:

  const prefAmount = Math.max(0, Math.min(capitalGains + dividends, taxableIncome));
  const capitalGainsTax = calcCapGainsTax(prefAmount, incomeTaxBase, p.capGainsBrackets[fs]);

Since incomeTaxBase = max(0, taxableIncome - capitalGains - dividends), this guarantees prefAmount + incomeTaxBase == taxableIncome, exactly mirroring the worksheet. Do NOT also cap the ltcgPlusQdiv passed to calcAmtTentative — that path already caps internally at taxableAmti (amt.ts line 49), and capping at regular taxable income there would be wrong because AMTI is a different base. Leave the AMT call (line 104-109) unchanged. Add a regression test: MFJ 2026, ordinary 20k, LTCG+QDI 100k, std deduction → assert capitalGainsTax == 0 (currently $120).

### ✅ 6. 🟠 QBI deduction disallowed in AMTI — AMTI overstated by the full QBI deduction — FIXED (c493eecd)

**Domain:** amt · **Severity:** high · **Category:** incorrect-calculation · **Location:** `src/lib/tax/calculate.ts:93-102`

- **Current behavior:** AMTI is built from taxableIncomeBeforeQbi (line 102: `const amti = taxableIncomeBeforeQbi + stdDeductionAddBack + (input.isoSpread ?? 0)`), which is taxable income BEFORE the §199A QBI deduction is subtracted (line 60/74: taxableIncome = taxableIncomeBeforeQbi − qbiDeduction). The comment on line 93 claims 'QBI IS allowed for AMT, so starting before QBI is correct' — but starting before QBI means the QBI deduction is never subtracted from AMTI, i.e. QBI is DISALLOWED for AMT. The logic in the comment is inverted relative to what the code does. Net effect: amti = taxableIncome + qbiDeduction + addbacks, overstating AMTI by exactly the QBI deduction.
- **Correct behavior:** The §199A QBI deduction IS allowed in computing AMTI, so AMTI should be reduced by it. AMTI should be built from taxableIncome (post-QBI) plus the std-deduction add-back and ISO spread — i.e. `taxableIncome + stdDeductionAddBack + isoSpread`, NOT taxableIncomeBeforeQbi.
- **Tax-law basis:** IRC §199A(f)(2): 'the deduction under subsection (a) shall be allowed for purposes of determining alternative minimum taxable income under section 55.' Form 6251 begins from Form 1040 taxable income (line 15), which is already net of the QBI deduction (Form 1040 line 13), and Part I has no QBI add-back line.
- **Impact:** Bites every client with a QBI (§199A) deduction. AMTI is overstated by up to 20% of qualified business income (the deduction can be tens of thousands of dollars), inflating tentative minimum tax and, for business owners near the AMT crossover, spuriously triggering or overstating AMT additional. Error on amtAdditional ≈ 26–28% × qbiDeduction in years where AMT binds.
- **Suggested fix:** In src/lib/tax/calculate.ts line 102, change `const amti = taxableIncomeBeforeQbi + stdDeductionAddBack + (input.isoSpread ?? 0);` to `const amti = taxableIncome + stdDeductionAddBack + (input.isoSpread ?? 0);` so the §199A QBI deduction (already netted out in taxableIncome at line 74) flows through to AMTI. Correct the misleading comment on lines 91-93 to read that QBI IS allowed for AMT, so AMTI starts from taxable income AFTER QBI (Form 6251 line 1 begins from Form 1040 taxable income, which is net of QBI). Add a regression test in calculate.test.ts: an MFJ client with a QBI deduction and no other preferences should produce an AMTI ordinary base equal to its regular incomeTaxBase plus any std-deduction add-back (i.e. QBI fully reflected in AMTI), and tentative AMT must drop versus the pre-fix value by ~26% × qbiDeduction when the base is below the 28% breakpoint.

### ✅ 7. 🟠 No SALT (state/local tax) add-back to AMTI for itemizers — FIXED (e2b7dea6; IRC §56(b)(1)(A)(ii), threaded capped SALT from belowLine.taxesPaid)

**Domain:** amt · **Severity:** high · **Category:** accuracy-improvement · **Location:** `src/lib/tax/calculate.ts:94-102`

- **Current behavior:** For itemizers, belowLineDeductions equals the full itemized total (which includes SALT — state income tax + property tax, capped) and stdDeductionAddBack is 0 (line 101). So amti = taxableIncomeBeforeQbi + 0 + isoSpread, leaving the entire SALT deduction embedded in AMTI with no add-back. The code comment explicitly acknowledges 'No SALT add-back for itemizers — SALT isn't tracked as a separate input', but SALT IS in fact tracked: src/engine/types.ts DeductionBreakdown.belowLine.taxesPaid (and stateIncomeTax/propertyTaxes) hold the capped SALT figure; calculate.ts simply never receives or uses it.
- **Correct behavior:** Taxes deducted on Schedule A (state/local income, sales, and property taxes) are NOT deductible for AMT and must be added back to taxable income when constructing AMTI. AMTI for an itemizer should be taxableIncome(post-QBI) + SALT deducted + isoSpread.
- **Tax-law basis:** IRC §56(b)(1)(A)(ii) — no deduction allowed for taxes described in §164(a) (state/local income, property, sales taxes) in computing AMTI; Form 6251 line 2a 'Taxes from Schedule A line 7.'
- **Impact:** Bites high-income itemizers in high-tax states — the single most common real-world AMT trigger. With the §164 SALT cap at (post-2025) levels this is up to the capped SALT amount per year of understated AMTI; at the 26/28% TMT rate the engine can fail to flag AMT for clients who would actually owe it, understating totalFederalTax by thousands.
- **Suggested fix:** Add an optional saltDeducted field to CalcInput (src/lib/tax/types.ts), thread deductionBreakdownResult.belowLine.taxesPaid through year-tax.ts into it, and in calculate.ts construct the AMT add-back so it covers BOTH the standard-deduction case and the itemizer SALT case:

  const amtAddBack = belowLineDeductions === stdDeduction
    ? stdDeduction
    : (input.saltDeducted ?? 0);

### 16. 🟠 Bracket-fill fixed-point iteration diverges/overshoots in the Social-Security 85% phase-in zone (slope ~1.85)

**Domain:** roth-conversions · **Severity:** high · **Category:** incorrect-calculation · **Location:** `src/engine/projection.ts:3171-3176 (sizeFillBracketConversion); mirrored in src/engine/roth-conversions.ts 328-335`

This implicitly assumes each $1 of conversion raises incomeTaxBase by exactly $1 (slope 1). When Social Security is in the 50%→85% phase-in band, each additional $1 of conversion raises taxable SS by up to $0.85 (ssTaxability.ts:35-37), so the marginal slope of incomeTaxBase is ~1.85. Capped at 6 iterations, the result lands far from the ceiling. I reproduced this directly against the engine: with SS held in the phase-in band throughout the fill, the conversion overshot the 22% ceiling (201,050) by $38,805 in the unit path, and the LIVE projection (joint loop) overshot by $8,256 at $160K combined SS and $18,439 at $180K combined SS, with the marginal tier landing at 24% instead of 22%. - **Correct behavior:** The solver must converge to incomeTaxBase ≈ ceiling regardless of the local slope. A secant/Newton step that estimates the local slope from two probe evaluations and updates `target += delta / slope` converges in a single iteration even at slope 1.85 (verified). Alternatively, damp the slope-1 update or raise the iteration cap to ~40 and detect oscillation. The conversion must not push the household past the targeted bracket ceiling when SS taxability is the cause of the steep slope.
- **Tax-law basis:** IRC §86 / IRS Pub 915 SS taxability worksheet: in the upper tier, taxable SS increases $0.85 per $1 of other income until 85% of benefits are taxed, giving a combined marginal base slope up to 1.85. Bracket boundaries per the OBBBA-permanent §1 schedule (2026 MFJ 22% ceiling).
- **Impact:** Bites high-SS households (e.g. two spouses each ~$45K+ SS) whose entire bracket-fill stays in the 85% phase-in band. ### 17. 🟠 Dual-entitlement (own + spousal) combined wrong: max(reducedOwn, reduced50%) instead of reducedOwn + reducedExcess
